Bird Song in Bijlmermeer
The Poetry and Politics of Intimate Entanglements
Nadia Al Issa / Christidi
Writer, arts practitioner, and researcher, History, Anthropology, and Science, Technology, and Society (HASTS) at MIT, USA

“What can bird singing competitions organized by Surinamese-origin communities in Bijlmermeer tell us about colonialism, indentured labour, and transnational migration?”

This was the question that researcher, writer and art practitioner Nadia Al Issa / Christidi posed to the 2018 Open Set Summer School group. In her lecture at the opening symposium Fluid Rhythms, Nadia elaborated upon this question and introduced the audience to the framework that guided the Summer School project, entitled I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings.

This project engaged with the tradition of bird singing competitions as an entry point into exploring colonial histories, postcolonial experiences, migration and community building, culture in translation, and the circulation of accompanying things (i.e. the economies of the bird trade, breeding, and betting). The group was invited to seek out ecosystems in Bijlmer that were of interest to them, through which they could similarly narrate the historical and political on a micro level, on the level of the everyday, as well as scale it up to the bigger picture of global thematics.

As Christidi introduces in her lecture, she approached the theme of rhythms through the idea of motion: through how, whether voluntarily or not, the rhythms of people and things simultaneously inhabit, develop in, or adapt to shared territories, conditions, and politics, and furthermore, how they influence each other and their shared context. To help us understand her perception of rhythm through motion, Christidi presented several key concepts, which were also the leading references for her Summer School workshop. These concepts invite us to focus on minor or neglected movements in culture and nature—the rhythms which were predominant in the research of the overall Open Set programme Fluid Rhythms.

The first of these key concepts is the idea of ‘minor histories’ (Ann Laura Stoler), which refers to incidents, movements and interactions that open up for us the ‘structure of feelings’ that might have been neglected by the major historical narratives. An alternative historical narration is proposed to be made through the rhythms of ‘minor events’, by taking these events as points of references. Secondly, Christidi introduces the notion of ‘ruination’ (Stoler), an act of motion from the colonial aftermath’s traces on the material environment and on people's bodies and minds. This is seen as an ongoing act: a process that we inhabit. Thirdly, Christidi draws our attention to Anna Tsing’s understanding of ‘friction’ in relation to fluidity. The tension between different rhythms, or friction, is on one hand a necessary element for initiating a movement, but on the other hand, it slows down fluid movement or changes the direction of the movement. Finally, the concept of ‘ruderal ecologies’ (Bettina Stoetzer) looks at the nature that grows on the cracks of urban ruins. This opens up the perspective of the moderation of some rhythms. Regardless of the attempt to control nature, ruderal ecologies speak about its adaptation or survival—even if a plant, grass or other life matter is conditioned by the urban structure, it’s not completely determined by it.

In her talk, Nadia Christidi contextualizes these theoretical frameworks through her practice, which consists of PhD research, essays and visual narratives.

Nadia Al Issa / Christidi is a Syrian, Palestinian, and Greek researcher, writer, and arts practitioner based in Cambridge, MA. Her exhibitions include: Untitled (8 km – A Tribute to Danis Tanović) (2011) in Beirut Art Center’s Exposure 2011; B is for Botany as I Is for Identity and Cultivating Exile (2015) in Apricots from Damascus at SALT, Galata, Istanbul; and Outside the Sentence There was a City at SALT, Ulus, Ankara. Nadia holds a BA in History of Art from Bryn Mawr College, Pennsylvania (2006) and an MA in Historical Studies from the New School for Social Research, New York (2015). She is currently a PhD student in History, Anthropology, and Science, Technology, and Society (HASTS) at MIT. While previously focused on nationalism, identity, and geopolitically contested spaces, her present work looks at the politicization of nature and the naturalization of politics.
