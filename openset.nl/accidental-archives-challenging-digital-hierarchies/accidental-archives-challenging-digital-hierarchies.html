Interview with Jonas Lund
Artist
28 November 2017, via Skype, between Design Academy Eindhoven and Paris, FR

Jonas Lund is a difficult man to pin down. The 34-year-old Swedish artist is, ostensibly, based in Berlin and in Amsterdam. But in reality, he is often out of the country for talks and exhibitions, making organising an interview quite a challenge.

Interest in Lund’s work has grown alongside a blossoming cultural scepticism towards technology, data and the internet — subjects that are often the focus of his provocative and wide-ranging work. Examples include Gallery Analytics, where he tracked the movements of visitors around a gallery and made the data publicly available, and The Fear of Missing Out, where an algorithm analysed works by successful contemporary artists and created instructions for making art, which was then displayed. For the 2014 project Studio Practice), Lund turned a gallery into a production line with workers making art from an instruction book and being assessed by a panel before being signed, all broadcast live via a website. More recent works include Away on Vacation — a live stream of his MacBook Pro running a piece of software that creates paintings by scripting the movement of the cursor and keyboard — and Critical Mass, a space that changes based on user actions and feedback submitted live via a website.

After a series of missed emails, re-scheduled appointments, and technological glitches, we finally sat down to talk via Skype while Lund was in Paris for a lecture and the ‘Critical Mass’ exhibition at École Municipale des Beaux-Arts/Galerie Édouard-Manet.

Jonas Lund

Eventually! It took a while! How’s it going?

Anna Winston

Not too bad, it’s been a long day and I guess the same for you.

JL

Yeah, I’m pretty tired so let’s see if I have some smart answers for you.

AW

Maybe we could start with you introducing yourself?

JL

Ok, so I will say it like this: [Ironic voice] Jonas Lund is Swedish artist who creates paintings sculpture, photography, websites and performances that critically reflect on contemporary network systems and power structures. He was born in Sweden and is based in Berlin and Amsterdam.

AW

[Laughs] Does the theme of Open Set Lab 2017, Memories of the Future, reflect the things that you are interested in at the moment?

JL

Memories of the Future — it’s pretty vague in some sense. You can project many different things towards it. If you’re talking about an archive as a concept, that’s something which is an innate quality of my work, because most of the works produce some kind of archive or documentation, so in that sense it’s relevant. It would be a bit of a stretch to say that that’s my main concern, but there’s something about it which is quite prevalent, let’s say. It’s an integral part of most artistic practices, whether that’s purely for documentation purposes or part of a creative practice or process of how you make something.

AW

One of the things that is so interesting about your work is that it’s quite hard to label. But could we talk about your work, in part, as a critical, digital archival practice?

JL

It’s a pretty funny one. I don’t necessarily disagree. I think it’s all a question of how specific you want to become and I think in quite a lot of my work, the basis for it is pretty specific. In that sense, the work itself can become a reflection of a certain technology or a certain structure or a certain behaviour in the online digital technical sphere. And by the mere fact of producing that work it also becomes an archive of that event.

In the example of Flip City — which is fun to bring up because it’s not very digital — this painting series with GPS trackers to address a flip market, this flip market doesn’t exist anymore in the same capacity. It’s silly to talk about historical reference points when it’s just a couple of years ago, nevertheless it becomes a historical point that could enter into some kind of archive, or maybe the work itself becomes a reflection of their position and that becomes the archiving process. So for sure it can be like this. I had some other things on my mind, but I lost the second point! Shit.

AW

Don’t worry! It’s interesting that you said that a couple of years ago doesn’t feel like history, but in the kind of media that you work with a couple of years can be quite a long time.

JL

Yeah, it’s a very long time and it’s not a very long time. It’s both. Supposedly technology and innovation move very quickly and a lot of new things emerge, but then the same basic systems of control and hierarchy still exist. I’m not actually interested in specific technologies. It’s kind of the opposite. It’s more the effects of technology, it’s what it produces, the subjectivity it informs if you have to spend all of your waking hours staring at Facebook. So, it’s both. In my work, it’s always double — everything has two interpretations, two meanings, two points of departure. You can be a fan [of technologies like Facebook], you can approve, and you can think ‘oh yeah that’s interesting’, or you can be extremely critical. 

AW

In a previous interview, you said that artists shouldn’t preach their opinions about their own work. Does this relate to this double meaning in your work?

JL

It’s more of a position to say that. Very few works function like that, because it’s more [like] propaganda where you’re trying to directly influence someone’s opinion. So it’s all very ambiguous in some sense. Which is also then employed as a strategy or as a way of showing that there are multiple readings of everything.

In Western society, ambiguity is typically seen as a weakness, because you can’t choose between this and that. But from the psychoanalysis point of view, ambiguity and being in an ambiguous state is just evidence of a very strong conflict. There is very strong conflict between all the different binary positions in some super strange way.

I think what I meant to say, in saying that artists shouldn’t push their points of view, is that the works I like mostly don’t. You have to leave some type of magic in there. If it’s too preachy it’s just tedious.

AW

I know you trained originally as a photographer…

JL

[Laughs] That’s true!

AW

And then you got into programming. I wonder if there’s still some kind of relationship between the way you see the world as a photographer and the way you are now using that as a lens onto something quite different.

JL

Hmmm, that’s a good question! I don’t think in my work that the method is that similar to when I was using photography as a tool. Photography felt like a limitation rather than an opportunity. Because you are stuck with this device that will gaze upon the world. It will always end up as a picture. But, for sure, there are similarities in the sense that you always work with tools that can enable different types of perceptions and different types of readings in what you see.

There are only a few contemporary artists that are solely photographers. Most of them are old. And German. And they’re fantastic artists, like Thomas Struth and Candida Höfer. But, of course, there’s new and interesting types of photography enabled by [the fact that] everybody has a camera anyways, so… What’s your point of view? Did you see any good photography works in the last five years that were super contemporary, that were amazing?

AW

I don’t know that I have. But that’s interesting because it’s only relatively recently that we’ve accepted photography as an art form rather than as a disposable medium. And I guess you’re working with types of media that also haven’t always been accepted as art...

JL

All the major museums in the world are starting to collect different types of online works and they’ve done multiple exhibitions on this now. This whole conservatism for the medium in the art world is kind of over. All that old-fashioned shit of ‘this is accepted and that’s not’, that’s over. The world changed pretty quickly. Everyone has an iPhone in their pocket, so do you really want to care about if photography is an art form or not, should that be your concern? Probably not, right?

AW

In terms of the relationship with museums and the ‘establishment’, if you have a project like Critical Mass, for example, or some of the projects that have involved a lot of live participation, does the idea of it being collected change it?

JL

Welllll, yes, I think in some sense. It depends very much on what it is, but underlying most of the work is some set of rules which kind of functions as a set of instructions. I know from my friends who work primarily in performance, that if they would sell work to a private collector or a museum, it basically comes as a set of instructions for restaging, so I think that’s the smart solution. But so far, I haven’t really been in that position where I have to reflect on it properly.

AW

Do works like Fear of Missing Out and Gallery Analytics relate to a kind of social anxiety around different expressions of digital culture?

JL

I suppose... I’ve noticed in the last two years this question appears much more frequently than before. And I think that’s already an indication of what’s on people’s minds, that everybody understands — slowly, maybe, maybe faster — that it’s not what it’s made out to be. The neoliberal Californian ideology, that technology will fix everything, is not the case. It does terrible things to societies in many parts of the world. And it does shit for politics. And it’s useless for democracy.

Not all of it, of course — there again there is nuance. But… there is definitely some type of discomfort. I think it’s only increasing. Maybe that will lead people to quit Facebook eventually. I don’t know how many studies you have to read about the use of Facebook and your personal happiness before you quit. Five of them, or ten of them, or 20?

All these false promises of social media giving you what you want, when you want it, and it’s going to make you great. Mark Zuckerberg says that as long as you give everyone a voice the world usually ends up in good place, which is also horseshit.

But I always felt like I had proper agency. By some virtue of privilege, I happened to get interested in learning how to write code. I can understand better than the average person, perhaps, the systems of control, how they function. I usually quote Douglas Rushkoff, although I don’t necessarily agree with it 100%. He says program or be programmed. So, either you learn how to program or you’re going to be programmed by the systems that govern society. It doesn’t mean you have to learn how to program, it just means you have to learn to recognise how certain systems favour certain behaviour, or how certain biases are integral to different systems online. Whether that’s Google PageRank or Facebook EdgeRank or whatever. It’s not objective. Once you get that, it gets easier to unravel more of this fuzzy logic they try and sell to you. Something like that? That sounds like there were some good quotes in that paragraph.

AW

Hah! One of the things I find interesting in what you do is that there’s an element of distribution built into the work. Is that a conscious decision, or does the work just evolve that way?

JL

I think it’s something you’re often confronted with in some way, how you contextualise and how you position your work. How it is mediated, basically. That was always a concern, even if you were a painter way back in the day, because part of the distribution is also the reception of the work — how does it satisfy a potential client or whatever. It’s just more on steroids today, where the artist is also supposed to be their own branding specialist and you’re also supposed to be the PR, the art critic and a self-curator, so you know everything about everything, and you know how to write very long theoretical texts, and you know how to get 200 likes on Instagram, you know how to reach out to magazines, and you know how to answer smart, complicated questions in interviews.

AW

[Laughs]

JL

It’s just become much more professionalised in general, the whole art scene. It’s very difficult to get anywhere by being naive. Maybe it’s a shame, but I think in every different industry it’s the same because, through the networked effect, competition is just increased and the speed with which we share information or get information is also massively increased.

At the heart of all my work is the network. Whether that’s a simplified, isolated network between two things, or a massive network between thousands of different nodes, part of the network is also the distribution model — how can you benefit from a networked effect of people wiring or thinking or exploring the works. So, I think it’s something that is embedded in all of it. It’s just there.

AW

Is the relationship in this kind of networked society that you describe, between the archive, production and distribution, becoming increasingly complicated?

JL

Yeah, for sure. Those different topics of mediation, distribution, production, archiving, they’re all kind of one, but separate. So, it’s difficult to tell where the line goes. I think I look more at the archive like a vast database which you have for yourself, but I’m not sure.

AW

You could talk about data as a form of archive…

JL

Yeah. I think the largest ongoing archive right now is Facebook. The intimate details of 2 billions people’s lives for the last like, I dunno, eight years. If you want to think about mankind in terms of that, it’s pretty crazy.

AW

It might also be interesting to think about how this is changing, because the Facebook demographic is getting older and younger people don’t engage with Facebook like that anymore and instead use these peer-to-peer messaging systems.

JL

Thank god. I mean, it’s about time that Facebook is disrupted, right?

AW

But then I wonder about the role of something like Instagram, or Flickr even, in this exhibition of the self. So rather than just having the data collected, you’re selecting what data you want to present.

JL

Yeah, for sure. It’s a strategy in some sense. It’s chasing some dopamine fix of getting liked, of being liked. I’m not sure what the implications are. Is the implication that your Instagram archive will make you happy, and show you the good things that happened to you in the last year, or will it be a reflection of all the shit that happened that you didn’t post? Is the end goal to have increased happiness? Is that we’re all looking for?

AW

Do you think artists have a responsibility to develop works that help people engage with a more thoughtful approach to digital culture?

JL

I don’t think so. I don’t think it makes sense to claim responsibility by saying ‘oh artists should do this or that’. The role of art in society is to comment on certain things or inform people.

First of all, I’m highly sceptical of the idea that art informs public opinion. Hardly ever. That doesn’t mean it doesn’t have a function. I think one of the functions can be to [examine] in new and imaginative and innovative ways how we’re getting fucked over. One of the functions of art can be to comment on society, and I think that’s definitely an important part. It’s something I find very interesting in my point of view because at the end of the day it’s just about what it’s like to be human, no? It’s like art is like ‘this is just what it’s like’, and then you have to take that as a starting point. But I wouldn’t say it’s our responsibility. I would say it’s the politicians’ responsibility to listen to the people and create regulations to have a counter to the neoliberal agenda. That’s 100% true, that you need to start to regulate shit to take back control. Which is happening. Maybe not as fast as we would like to see it. But, nevertheless, it is happening.

AW

In the context of all of this, how do you decide what you’re going to develop for your next project?

JL

By complete random chance! No, I don’t know. I mean it’s hard to say because it really depends. The word that everybody loves in the art world is ‘urgency’, so then it’s about what’s the most efficient use of your agency to capture the most urgent point. But in the end I don’t want to be overly strategic. It’s more like a feeling, or to explore something unknown or test a new idea, which could lead to something else. Maybe you will take the topic that will make you the least depressed. How do you decide what you’re going to research next?

AW

Um, I read a lot and then if I find there’s a few different things that interest me in a similar way, then that is my next line of inquiry.

JL

That’s a good answer! Yeah, I like it. I also read a lot. Yeah. OK, I read a lot. And then I think about it and then as all of these different readings emerge and they diverge, they reach a conclusion of amazingness and then boom, there it is! [Laughs] Yeah, I’m not sure. But you do consume a crazy amount of input and then you also have to stop for a while and go back to output I think. It’s a weird combination.

AW

Are you ever surprised by the way your work is received or interpreted?

JL

I mean, I don’t think about it too much. What I find most interesting about it is the conversations that come out of it. Then it can lead somewhere else. I try and not care so much about if people like it or not, because I don’t want to use that as a metric for evaluating what’s successful or not, but of course it plays a part too. I usually say I don’t care so much about audience, which may be true or not. See? It’s all double! In some regards, yeah, it may be interesting to hear, in other regards it’s completely, completely irrelevant. It all depends on the purpose.

AW

With your workshop at Open Set Lab 2017, what’s the one thing that you would hope people would take away for themselves and their work?

JL

Oh, that’s a tricky one. An enlightened sense of agency within online systems, let’s say. I mean, I don’t know. It’s a bit silly to say that their biggest takeaway would be to be much more sceptical about what’s being sold to you, because I imagine that most Open Set participants already have a pretty high level of scepticism about different types of power structures. But you’ve got to stay vigilant and not get fooled by fake news. It’s quite difficult. Actually, it’s really difficult. It’s also about recognising the difficulties of manoeuvring within this landscape I think.

AW

Thank you very much, I really enjoyed this conversation.

JL

Alright cool, no problem. Ciao.  

Originally from London, Anna Winston is an editor, writer and curator with more than 10 years’ experience in architecture, design and technology, including her former role as editor of Dezeen, the world’s biggest architecture and design site. She now splits her time between London, Antwerp and Eindhoven, where she is currently researching the future of augmented reality and public space. She is also working on a project focusing on the design of death.

Art as a Public Issue Jorinde Seijdel
For a long time, the public sphere as a space in which rational debates are conducted, free of prescriptive forces, and public space as a common world were guiding concepts in the discourse on publicness defined by such thinkers as Jürgen Habermas and Hannah Arendt. These enlightened forms of ‘civilized publicness’ seem far removed from either the theory or the practice of the present day. Neoliberal forces, such as privatization and commercialization, are torpedoing the idealized modern concepts of the public sphere, which is being increasingly defined, in terms of a practical project, by acute expectations concerning security and threat. At the same time, public space is being claimed by groups and audiences such as illegal aliens, refugees and migrants, who are not accounted for, or only minimally, in official policy dealing with this space.
