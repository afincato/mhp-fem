A Truth to Deviate From
Esteban Gomez-Rosselli
Interview with Noam Toran
Artist
29 November 2017 via a video call between Design Academy Eindhoven and Rotterdam, NL

In his lecture for Open Set Lab 2017, the artist Noam Toran discussed the transgressive potential of alternative narratives and para-literature, in the context of the vast audio-visual archive, where the lecture took place. He raised questions about how misreading and misinterpreting the archive in a conscious manner might result in the creation of fictions that allow for the generation of alternative perspectives, and a more complex understanding of reality.

Toran’s work encompasses films, performances, exhibitions — the work is less about concrete results than it is about orchestrating situations through which themes, usually those that question social conformity, take voice through objects and narratives. Toran resists labelling himself as a critical designer or an artist, focusing instead on how his work can explore historical periods and objects, and their influences. He insists that memory and fiction are not really two things apart, and that the lines between them can blur to revale new and unexpected perspectives.

Esteban Gomez Rosselli

Hey Noam.

Noam Toran

Hey Esteban. Can you see me? Oh no, wait, I need to turn the cam on.

Can you hear and see me now?

EGR

Perfectly. Can you tell me what you’re working on at the moment?

NT

Uhm… what am I doing at the moment? I have a show at my gallery in Tel Aviv for next year. That’s really the major project in front of me and I’m working on a project that in some ways is a continuation of what I showed in the introduction to my Open Set project, where I’m producing a kind of science fiction play about the real workers union in Palestine in the 1930s, so in the pre-independence British mandate Palestine. So, I’m turning to a lesser-known period and drawing out a piece of historical material that I think it’s relevant, to try and understand where Israel went wrong, politically and ideologically.

EGR

How do you decide what historical moment interests you in order to begin a project like that?

NT

I think it starts with something that has been pulling me towards some area of interest. But it’s also something localized, a focused context, which helps in the telling of a story, and means that a story can be quite accurate. If the characters in it are already historically and geographically located somewhere, and there is some flavour to the time I’m looking at, this informs my aesthetic decisions. So, for me, it’s about finding an accurate space to work with and then drawing it out into the narrative structures that are connected to science fiction1 or ghost fiction or something like that to try to layer that moment with a kind of greater perspective, be it historical, ideological or otherwise.

EGR

You were born in New Mexico, you grew up in San Francisco, and you have Jewish ancestry. Now you live in Rotterdam. Do you think these different cultural or transcultural perspectives affect the way you confront historical materials?

NT

Possibly, yeah. I think I grew up in a pretty liberal-minded, politically very leftist family and so, if I learned anything through my experience and education, it has been to really embrace the complexity of historical political circumstances and not to consider things in the sharp contrast of black and white; that there are complexities, that there are hypocrisies and that there are problematics that are not just a binary condition. Trying to understand a range of experiences, a range of ingredients that produce a political and historical cocktail is something that I have always accepted.

EGR

Considering your awareness of the complexity of political situations — and your acknowledgement that archival material has already been infected by ideological conditions — what is the best way to approach the archival material and confront it in an environment like the Netherlands Institute for Sound and Vision?

NT

I think an environment like the Netherlands Institute for Sound and Vision is amazing at every level — that building, that space. It is an amazing example of a ‘national narrative’ being told, where we, as outsiders, really even at the level of the language, we are coming to it as a piece of science fiction. Our only tangible connection to this narrative is through this dignified museum, where we recognize the framework — we have been to museums like that elsewhere — but the content is totally alien to us. The puppets are not the puppets of our own childhoods. So, this idiosyncratic nation-specific material becomes some kind of a weird, distorted hallucinatory version of what we know from our own childhoods. I think that the Institute is already questioning how a nation perceives itself through media. As outsiders, we are in a really fortunate situation because we are not invested in the material nostalgically or otherwise. We don’t care, we don’t know anything about it, and we come to it as aliens, and that perspective is critical to provide a kind of postcolonial reading of such material. Often such material tends to under-represent, let’s say, immigrant groups or minority groups of importance in the cultural, and even physical, building of a nation. They tend to be marginalized in media so it’s interesting to extract these small stories and blow them up so people can see them.

EGR

In that line of thought, to bounce back you’re brief to you- 2

NT

That is lazy journalism Esteban, bouncing questions... [laughing]

EGR

OK: What opportunities arise when misreading the archive, what is the potential of these things from the past to modify the future?

NT

It’s to provide a different reading of the material, and again if that reading is done through a filter of foreignness, or otherness, then I think it has value. If the Institute for Sound and Vision creates a cathedral-like framework where we walk into this space that is heavily coded with this kind of scale and rendering of how material is digested either by citizens coming to the museum or by researchers, we can also create another frame within this sci-fi building in which we can examine how Holland renders itself through these institutions.

EGR

And is this how you framed your brief for the Open Set participants?

NT

My initial proposition was really a way to consider an approach to the archive but it wasn’t a brief in itself. A lot of the participants already have their own ideas of how they want to get involved with the material and my role is more as kind of a support mechanism helping them to consider this approach. Last time we met they showed me what they were thinking about and I helped them consider ways to produce the material, to turn the research of the material into something that may be considered a finished film or product in some kind.

EGR

Do you think this disruptive engagement with archival material has special relevance today in the times of Google and massively available content with algorithmic categorization, one click away, responding to our habits?

NT

Yes and no. Google, YouTube… these are the great archives of the world. These citizen-based uploading practices are remarkable and, in a way, it’s up to us to produce the kind of taxonomy we want in order to use the material from a critical perspective. In this case we need to have something like a ‘national archive’, which defines itself using structures such as having to collect everything that is broadcast. What I also like about that approach is that is must engage with the full spectrum of material — both highbrow and lowbrow material — what is considered culturally acceptable within the frameworks of television. The British Library is required by law to collect every publication produced in the UK, and consequently ends up having the largest collection of pornography in the world! That produces this wonderful pink haze in a corner of the British Library — this cornucopia of pornography maybe metres away from the Gideon Bible or something; it’s quite amazing to consider the ironies of that.

[Skype notifies someone went online]

EGR

You were talking about the ironies of accepting lowbrow materials...

NT

What’s important is that it is all relevant and once we consider that it is up to us... You know some of the greatest cultural and political theorists are those that can extract from what seem like irrelevant ways to understand the society they are looking into. We can learn from those kinds of writers and theorists. I’m more drawn to the lowbrow material — be it film, or TV, or comics, or kids TV programs — precisely to understand the spectrum of society and television that are not always valued.

EGR

And on the impact of media, of this cinematographic moodiness…

NT

Definitely, the ability to steer us as viewers. The emotional registers are an important part of what is considered the format. And some of it is really just trickery, you know, and that is nice to consider. And it is nice to participate, when producing the work, by using the tricks of the trade. A lot of the Open Set participants are actually really interested in deconstructing the codes of production — how something is made and what tricks are being used. When we look at documentary, for example, it can use devices such as an overbearing soundtrack to tug at the heartstrings. All these things should be considered when watching, and it’s really nice that the participants are after that.

EGR

With this trickery in production, what do you find fascinating about the way that format can reshape memory?

NT

Trickery or otherwise I think, from my own experience, I have a more porous memory than others — our revisiting of ourselves in the past tends still to be for the most part visual. There might be one aspect of it that is very true to that moment. The rest we fill in, because we need to, because we are not that satisfied; we are seeing this tiny fragment we render in the screen of an overall perspective. We don’t really remember, we fill it in, often with material that has been given to us through media and that’s not really our own. Everything tends to get grainy and mushy. That grain, that mushiness, that muddiness is where the most interesting critical perspectives lie, where some of the most interesting works lie.

EGR

Do you have any particular fictions you constantly come back to?

NT

Yes, two short fiction writers I always return to are Jorge Luis Borges and Raymond Carver. I mean, I’m not a writer but if there is a kind of translation aspect to the kind of work that I do then it tends to concern narratives that have a short story format. So I am always returning to them3, even as technical artists, for what they are capable of producing in such a distilled format, the compression of the scenarios, the efficiency, the emotional resonances. Is that ok?

EGR

Yes fantastic, thank you Noam!

NT

How are you going to format this interview?

EGR

We are still discussing it, but want to engage with the raw transcripts to add different layers to them.

NT

Sounds like a great experience, I think.

EGR

Yes, we hope so.

Esteban Gomez Rosselli studied design at Universidad de los Andes in Bogotá, where he focussed mostly on design ecosystems, signification processes, and interpersonal moments of contact. He is now pursuing his interests in cultural analysis focusing on design and the way it generates complex relations of meaning.
